We are entering a world where execution is increasingly cheap.

Need code? AI can generate it.
Need UI drafts? AI can produce ten in minutes.
Need tests, docs, PR summaries? Same story.

So if your entire professional value is "I implement fast," you are now competing with software that costs less than a team lunch.

That does not make engineers, designers, or QA irrelevant.
It means the differentiator has moved.

The people who stay valuable are the ones who can answer:

- What problem are we actually solving?
- Who feels that pain strongly enough to care?
- Is this feature meaningful, or just another button no one asked for?
- What is the business impact if we build this now versus later?

That mindset is product thinking.

Most products fail for a simple reason: teams solve the wrong problem with high quality.
They ship polished solutions to low-value pain.

You can have clean architecture, strong test coverage, and beautiful design and still lose because nobody truly needs what you built.

Product thinking helps you catch that early.
Before implementation.
Before design polish.
Before sprint after sprint disappears into output with no outcome.

This is why it is not a PM-only skill.
Developers need it.
Designers need it.
QA needs it.
Everyone building product needs it.

And despite all progress, AI still lacks one critical thing: real ownership of user context.
It can suggest.
It can simulate.
But it does not sit with your customer pain every day.
You do.

So where do you train this skill for real?

Work helps, but only partially.
In many teams, priorities are already set, timelines are fixed, and your role is mostly delivery.

The fastest way to build strong product judgment is to run your own project, ideally one with real users and real pricing.

When it is your product:

- you choose what to build,
- you decide what to cut,
- you face user confusion directly,
- and you feel the ultimate feedback loop: payment or no payment.

That last part changes everything.

Building features is one muscle.
Building something people will pay for is another.
